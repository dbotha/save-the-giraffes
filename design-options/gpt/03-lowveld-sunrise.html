About the Limpopo Giraffe Initiative

LGI is a registered non-profit organisation operating in Limpopo Province, working within private wildlife reserves to protect South Africa's giraffe populations. While giraffe numbers in the region are currently stable, they are far from secure. Fenced reserves create closed ecosystems where populations can quickly exceed the land's ability to sustain them. South African law requires reserves to maintain compliance with ecological carrying capacity — and when they can't, the consequences fall on the animals.

The Problem

Reserves must remove excess giraffe to stay within legal carrying capacity. But for smaller reserves, the costs are prohibitive.

Capture

Professional wildlife capture teams are expensive and require specialist equipment.

Vet Care

Every giraffe needs veterinary assessment and care before and during translocation.

Transport

Specialised vehicles and logistics to safely move giraffe between reserves.

Assessment

Carrying capacity assessments are legally required but costly to commission.

When reserves cannot afford these steps, the default outcome is culling — and selling the meat.

Our Solution

  • Fund the translocation of giraffe from overstocked reserves to suitable new homes.
  • Support carrying capacity assessments so reserves can plan ahead.
  • Enable smaller reserves to participate in ethical wildlife management.
  • Improve genetic diversity across fragmented populations.
  • Prevent culling by making relocation affordable and accessible.

Our Approach

Short Term

Relocate giraffe within South Africa — moving animals from overstocked reserves to those with capacity, preventing culling and strengthening herds across the country.

Long Term

Establish a regional sourcing centre for neighbouring countries, enabling cross-border giraffe conservation and supporting populations throughout southern Africa.

Proven Example

Working with Blyde Wildlife Reserve, we successfully relocated 11 giraffe to Mountainlands Nature Reserve near Barberton. This translocation improved genetic diversity at the destination reserve, strengthened both populations, and — most importantly — prevented the culling of healthy animals.

This is the model we aim to replicate across the region: science-led, funded relocation that saves lives and builds healthier ecosystems.
